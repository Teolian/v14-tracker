Methodology

This page documents how the numbers on the live tracker are produced. The tracker is a forward test: forecasts are committed to git before the weather happens and verified against physical sensors once observations are published. Nothing on the tracker is backfitted, re-run, or selected after the fact.

1. What is being forecast

Surface solar irradiance (GHI, W/m²) at 41 JMA pyranometer sites across Japan, hourly, for the next day. V15 is the current model — its live count started Jun 11, 2026; any earlier starred value is a back-cast/backfill computed on archived inputs and is always marked. V14 remains the long-running benchmark line. Only forecasts committed before the start of the target day are scored as live. Ground truth = the official JMA pyranometer archive, published after each day closes (~00:30 JST). Only daylight hours are scored (clear-sky irradiance > 10 W/m²).

2. Issue times and lead windows

Every forecast value has three timestamps: model init time, our issue time, and the valid hour being forecast. Lead = valid − issue. The tracker runs several daily issue slots; each comparison lives strictly inside one slot:

  • Day view (main table): forecast issued the previous evening (last run before 00:00 JST), leads ≈ 8–23 h to daylight hours.
  • Vendors comparison: pooled rows filtered to leads 12–36 h — the conventional «day-ahead» band used in the solar-forecasting literature, kept for comparability with published results.
  • Contract slot (accumulating): issued ~06:00 JST the day before delivery, leads ≈ 23–38 h — matches a real Japanese submission deadline.

3. Identical rows

All models in any table are scored on exactly the same rows: same station, same hour, same issue slot, truth present for all. The n shown in each row is the size of that common set. We never compare our forecast from one slot with a competitor from another.

4. Reference models

JMA MSM (5-km operational mesoscale model; we verified that our feed equals the raw government model output with correlation 0.989, median difference 0.4 W/m²), ECMWF IFS (0.25°), NOAA GFS (0.25°), and smart persistence — «tomorrow keeps today’s clear-sky index», the standard no-skill baseline of the field.

5. Metrics

  • RMSE — root-mean-square error; penalises large misses, which carry disproportionate cost in power markets. Used for day cells and the map.
  • MAE — mean absolute error; the typical hourly miss. Shown on station pages.
  • nRMSE — RMSE divided by the mean measured daytime irradiance of the same rows; makes results comparable across climates and seasons.
  • Skill = 1 − RMSEmodel/RMSEpersistence on identical rows; 0 = no better than naive, 1 = perfect. Day-ahead skill of 0.3–0.4 is considered strong in the literature.
  • P10–P90 coverage (PICP) — how often reality falls inside our published 80% uncertainty band; the honesty of the band, target 0.80.
  • Pinball loss — the standard quantile-forecast score (mean of the 0.1 and 0.9 pinball losses); how we compare uncertainty bands to each other.

Published daytime RMSE for the raw operational mesoscale model over Japan ranges roughly 95–170 W/m² across regions and seasons (Solar Energy 2012–2015; Energy Procedia 2014) — the colour scale of the tracker is anchored to that literature.

6. The V15 ribbon (P10–P90)

The shaded band on station pages is produced by hourly quantile models recalibrated daily against the latest verified observations — a rolling conformal scheme with a multi-week window, a publication-lag embargo and separate calibration per sky regime. The band aims to contain reality 80% of the time, and its width breathes: narrow on stable clear days, wide in changeable weather — that width is exactly the uncertainty a market participant should price. Rare quantile crossings are displayed sorted. Days computed on archived inputs are always marked (*backfill); the live parallel log started Jun 11, 2026. The previous 3-hourly band is still logged in parallel for honest comparison but is no longer displayed.

7. Provenance & reproducibility

Each forecast run is appended to a log and committed to git minutes after issuance — the commit timestamp precedes the forecast validity period. The site is rebuilt automatically once a day after JMA publishes observations; no manual step touches the numbers. Sample sizes are shown everywhere; early-period results (first weeks) should be read with their n in mind.
